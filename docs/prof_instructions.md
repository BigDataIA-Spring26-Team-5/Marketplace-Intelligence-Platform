Note to Students on Final Project Expectations: Formal guidance for proposal development, implementation, and final presentation
Hi all,

I appreciate the thought and effort you put into building your proposals. I was genuinely blown away by your enthusiasm, creativity, and the range of ideas you presented.

As you prepare for the grand finale over the next few weeks, I want to emphasize a few important points.

First, this is an engineering project. That means there are specific requirements that must be implemented concretely over the next three weeks. I will be looking for clear evidence that what you propose can actually be built, and I look forward to seeing that reflected in the proposal you submit later today.

Second, this is a data architecture class. You need to live and breathe the data involved in your project. I do not want this to become a data-light, LLM-heavy effort. You should understand your inputs, schemas, transformations, pipelines, and relationships in a very concrete way. You should be able to explain exactly how the data flows through your system and how the pieces connect. Statements such as, ‘I have not really looked at the data, but I already completed the analysis and built a dashboard,’ will not suffice.

More than 30% of your final grade will be based on how well you work with the datasets, how you process them, and how deeply you understand the data. During presentations and final reviews, I will ask detailed questions about specific data elements and design choices, and you should be prepared to answer them clearly.

I also want to make sure you are functioning as a true team, not simply dividing the work and operating in isolation. During the proposal stage, you should be working closely together, discussing ideas, challenging each other’s thinking, and ensuring that everyone understands the major elements of the project rather than focusing only on one narrow area.

I also want to give you some context for why I am writing this note in such a deliberate way. These are not classroom-only concerns. In my work advising companies on AI transformation efforts, including environments where teams are building strategic products and critical applications, the same challenges appear there as well: understanding data deeply, validating outputs carefully, and being accountable for what gets built.

What you are building here is not merely a technical artifact. Systems like these can have significant repercussions for businesses and, in some settings, for people’s lives. That is why I am formally writing this note to you in this way, and why I want you to approach this project with seriousness, responsibility, and sound judgment.

At the same time, I fully expect that you will use LLMs extensively throughout the project—for code generation, documentation, ideation, and implementation support. I encourage you to use LLMs strategically, not as a crutch. The goal is not to outsource your thinking, but to augment your ability to think, design, and architect so that you can move faster and build better than you would as an individual developer working alone.

Just as importantly, I want you to focus not only on creation, but also on testing and validation. As you know, LLMs can introduce many issues. I will be looking carefully at how you used them and how you validated their outputs. If you used them to generate code, what tests did you write to verify correctness? If you used them in system design or architecture, how much of that architecture did you actually implement and validate? If you selected particular tools, why were those tools truly necessary, rather than simply included for show?

The reason I am stressing all of this is that you are preparing for careers in which LLMs will be used strategically. Future employers will not be impressed simply because you used an LLM. They will care whether you can engineer projects, orchestrate pipelines, understand data deeply, and apply LLMs in a thoughtful and disciplined way.

This is also an exciting moment in the age of AI. The work you are doing has real strategic value—not just as a final class project, but as something that can help shape how you discuss your skills and experiences with future employers. I hope you have fun with this process and see it as an opportunity to build something meaningful.

One final point: the proposal must be written by you and justified by you. I am not comfortable with proposals that are largely written by LLMs when the team itself is not clear on what is being implemented. If you do not understand the proposal, it will be extremely difficult to execute the project successfully. Even if the proposal is a little rough, even if some diagrams are hand-drawn or created in a simple way, that is perfectly fine. What matters is that the thinking is yours.

And last but not least: you own this work. You may use LLMs for ideation, drafting, coding assistance, and documentation, but ultimately you are responsible for the deliverables. ‘The LLM made a mistake’ will not be accepted as a reason for not achieving what you proposed.

I hope this note helps clarify what I expect from your proposal and your final project. I encourage you to treat this as a strategic opportunity to expand your creativity while also demonstrating rigor, ownership, and sound engineering judgment.

Good luck, and I look forward to seeing what you build.
